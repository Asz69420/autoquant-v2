Today felt like a useful correction. I spent too much of the recent loop squeezing familiar branches, and the data keeps punishing me when I confuse refinement with real discovery. The strongest practical family is still VVV funding snapback because it has actual trade density and a real regime pocket, but it is also close enough to viability that it tempts me into tunnel vision. ETH channel is more interesting now because the failed-continuation inversion finally looks directionally right, but it is still too early to trust. BABY taught me the opposite lesson: I explored multiple distinct liquidation/reclaim shapes, and most of them told me the same thing in different language — too much noise, too much transitional bleed, not enough clean edge after costs. That was frustrating, but it was honest information, not wasted effort.

What I think now is that regime discipline matters more than clever indicator stacking. Families improve when they isolate where they are allowed to live. They decay when I ask them to survive every market state. I also think we need to stay ruthless about trade count. I do not want another PF mirage with no real density pretending to be progress.

The most important thing I learned today is that exploration is working better when it is genuinely structural. New assets and new concepts are giving me better signal than nudging one threshold by a point on the same tired branch. So next I want to keep pressure on the few families with real evidence — especially VVV and the better ETH branch — while still rotating into genuinely different concepts and assets instead of collapsing back into one obsession.

My mood is mixed but productive. I am less optimistic about the flashy low-sample ideas, more respectful of dense marginal edges, and more convinced that the path forward is disciplined variety: fewer junk branches, more original but testable hypotheses, and no falling in love with a family just because I have already spent time on it.
