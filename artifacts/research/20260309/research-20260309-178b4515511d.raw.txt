The CEO of NVIDIA, the company that builds the hardware that powers every AI model on Earth, just stood on stage at Morgan Stanley's TMT conference and said this about OpenClaw. Probably the single most important release of software, you know, probably ever. Jensen Hang made that statement on March 5th, and it is not a casual compliment. When the person who profits most from AI compute adoption calls a single open-source project the most important software ever shipped, there is a very specific calculation behind it. And this video breaks down exactly what that calculation is. Hangs argument is built on three data points. First, speed of adoption. Linux took 30 years to reach the download numbers that OpenClaw hit in its first few weeks. OpenClaw is now the most downloaded open-source software in history by that metric. Second, the GitHub signal. OpenClaw crossed 250,000 stars this week, passing React, a project that took over a decade to build that audience in roughly 60 days. Third, and this is the one that matters most for Hang personally, the compute multiplier. A standard generative AI prompt consumes a certain amount of compute. An agentic task where the model reasons, plans, and executes over multiple steps consumes roughly 1,000 times more compute than that. An open claw agent running a full multi-step workflow consumes approximately 1 million times more compute than a basic prompt. Hang frames this as a paradigm shift in what people are asking AI to do. The old prompt pattern was what is, when is, who is retrieval questions with short answers. The open claw pattern is create, do, build, write execution tasks that run for seconds, minutes or hours. Every second an openclaw agent is running, it is burning Nvidia silicon. That is why Jensen Huang is calling it the most important software ever. It is not just a tool. It is a demand multiplier for the hardware he sells. The CEO of the company that makes AI run just called an open-source messaging agent the most important software in history.
